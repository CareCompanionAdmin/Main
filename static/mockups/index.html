  • Mobile-native feel

02 · Nurturing Low density · warm

Care Garden

Each life domain is a "plant" the parent is tending — meds, sleep, mood, bright spots. Soft growth rings replace charts and big numbers. The dashboard celebrates what's logged rather than nagging about what isn't. Auto-detected positives bloom at the top.

  • Reframes "data entry" as care
  • Quick-add seeds for fast logging
  • Working meds bottom sheet to try
  • Metaphor isn't for everyone
  • Less suited to clinician sharing
  • Trends require drilldown

03 · Narrative Medium density

Today's Story

A journal-like vertical timeline of Emma's day, with a permanent "tell me about Emma…" composer at top. Logging is the conversation; review is the scroll. The most natural "what happened today" mental model.

  • Logging feels like talking

  • Emotional warmth
  • Trends harder to spot
  • Long scroll over time

04 · Letter-like Lowest density

Postcard Home

Once-a-day "postcard" the app sends you about your child. Watercolor pastel, hand-lettered greeting, three short neighborhoods (today's care · last night · this week's bright spot). Frames the data as a friend checking in, not a clipboard.

  • Most reassuring tone of the four
  • Mood picker + note save are wired
  • Reduces the "log everything" pressure
  • Not for power users
  • One direction at a time (today only)
  • Trend view requires a separate screen

What the research said

Caregiver burden is the #1 risk

The JAKE study (PMC6454343) found that caregivers gave 2–3 reports per week despite daily prompts. Long forms drove abandonment. Single-question mood capture (good day / bad day, weather icons, valence×arousal) outperformed long forms. Today's CareCompanion has 12 modal categories with 20–50 fields each — a textbook fatigue trap.

Visual metaphors beat text labels

Multiple studies on autism-app UX show icons and weather/emoji metaphors reduce comprehension load. All four mockups lean into this — sun and weather (1), garden plants and rings (2), journal stickers (3), watercolor and stamps (4) — instead of charts and acronyms.

Reframe data entry as care

Care-app research consistently shows that parents abandon when an app feels like a chart. Mockup 2 (Care Garden) reframes each life domain as a "plant" the parent is tending — logging becomes watering, not data entry. The dashboard celebrates what's logged rather than nagging about what isn't.

Reassurance over analytics

Stressed primary caregivers (typically the mother) need a daily "your child is okay" before they need a trend line. Mockup 4 (Postcard Home) treats the dashboard as a once-a-day note from a friend — three short paragraphs, hand-lettered greeting, watercolor pastels. Trends still exist; they just live a tap away rather than greeting you on open.

Calm-tech palette

Cool blues and greens evoke calm and trust; warm cream/amber feels safe and journal-like. Avoid heavy red except for genuine alerts. Today's app uses 11 category colors at equal saturation — visually loud. Each mockup picks a tighter palette.

Reduce notification fatigue

One well-timed external prompt outperformed multiple in-app interruptions in the JAKE 8-week trial. Implication: the current 5-button header (Logs / Meds / Alerts / Insights / Reports) competes for attention. All four mockups collapse it.

Tooling note

No Claude Code skill, plugin, or MCP server in your environment is specifically aimed at graphic/UX design — the installed superpowers, simplify, and update-config skills are dev-workflow oriented; the MCPs (Gmail, Drive, Calendar, CData) are auth/data only. So these mockups were hand-built from research findings rather than generated. Worth knowing if you wanted to chase a Figma-style tool — it'd be a separate add.
